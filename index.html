I was born in the period of Reconstruction of Le Havre. ln 1956 my parents found themselves being allocated a home which was well placed, in the centre of town - and with a sea view! This was the ideal formula for a young couple looking after four children and a grand-mother. Nevertheless we had to wait several months until the standard of comfort in this flat reached a satisfactory level. Indeed, the water supply often failed because of a lack of pressure; and we had to go down, from the sixth floor, to the basement - although there was a lift - to fill up the buckets with the water we needed for the day. we needed for the day. we needed for the day.

The noise of the pile-driver and the horns of the steam-boats orchestrated our afternoons at the Mailleraye school. ln the evening Mum would come to pick us up, and we’d go for a bit of relaxation down at the beach. ln the smaller port, at low tide, the smell of the slime would sometimes fill the air. So, we would quickly slope off to the sea-wall where the smells of seaweed and warm sand would welcome us.

ln front of us lay the grey expanse of beach; to our left the Olsen sea-wall with its fringes of rocks and, at the far end, the remains of a shipwreck. We’d take off our clothes and run to the water where other kids were shouting and throwing water over each other. The "biggies" would gather round large inner-tubes from which they’d leap into the sea before coming back to grab hold of them and shake out their wet hair. After that we would go foraging in the rocks, looking for little green crabs, which were plentiful at that time, and would delight in bursting the iodine-filled vessels of giant greeny-black seaweed. A whole swarm of children in bathing-trunks, some of those trunks knitted, would be milling about at the base of the sea-wall, splashing in the water left in the rock-pools.

At the water's edge were some men equipped with a sort of plastic pouch, worn on their hips, who were scraping at the sand with their "prodders", lifting them regularly to harvest the grey shrimps trapped in their nets. They moved along the beach in this way, as far as the breakwaters of Sainte-Adresse. The other favourite game consisted of building sand-castles before the all-devastating tide came back in; and for the smallest kids the challenge of filling the sand-castle moats with water! Preparing to go back, in the evening, we had to change behind the towel which Mum held up in front of us, rinse out our bathing-costumes in a final move towards the sea-shore and wipe our feet which were still covered with the sand which clung to them. Sometimes we had to scratch them with some shingle, followed by more sand, trying to remove the dirty oil picked up during our play and which stained our feet.

lf we weren't going to the Chapeau de Napoléon, and if the tide-table allowed it, Sundays were usually bathing days. The families would arrive with baskets containing the bathing-towels, rubber rings and toys for the children; and the best organised brought parasols. There were children everywhere, playing while their parents supervised them from a distance.

Around the middle of the afternoon the sweet-seller came along. You could hear his heavy foot-steps as he waded through the shingle, and his call would put us on alert: pains chocolat, pains au raisin, coconut biscuits! But usually we weren't buying anything from him, because we had our tea already catered for: casse-croûte Rogeron [a local Norman speciality] and chocolate or buttered bread with chocolate. The ice-cream seller also plied the beach on Sundays; but, for our part, we were going to be at Madame Miko's; she lived on the ground floor of the building. Thus, without getting bored, we spent hours with new discoveries, occasionally punctuated with small incidents.

One afternoon my little brother slipped away from our parents' watch. The whole family, in a state of turmoil, scoured the beach until we found the little "Toutounet" newly adopted by another clan and tucking with gusto into the cake they had offered him. Another time there was a piece of magic: a storm came in from the sea. We saw the sky cloud over with grey and felt the wind rise up. Like a flock of panicking birds, all the people on the beach broke away and ran. In our case, though, we had come that day with the little wigwam my little brother had got for Christmas! So Dad, Mum, Marc and I took refuge in this tent (which came with a ground-sheet); and we waited for the rain .... This seemed like an extraordinary spectacle to us; and the pleasure of being in there, in the comfort of that tent, the only ones protected from the storm, stays in my mind as an unforgettable moment.

The years passed; and adolescence arrived. Friends of my parents acquired a cabin at the maritime baths. lt was a classic cabin, an annexe of their home. The chip-pan took pride of place on the portable stove. The family would eat there at mid-day as the father came back from work, took his dip in the sea, had a rest and returned to the office, only to return at the end of the afternoon. That kind of pattern was widespread at the time. A table, loungers and a two-seater canoe would complete the kitting-out of the cabin.

We used to settle ourselves in, the younger generation arranging itself by age bracket. In the morning, as one arrived, the first chore was to go to the drinking-fountain, turn the lever and collect the water for the domestic needs of the day to come. We left out a bucket of water in the sun, to be able to have a tepid shower later. We would go to this water-fountain several times a day, to wash ourselves in soft water, to rinse out our bathing costumes or simply to drink from the spout...

Around the cabins the atmosphere differed from on the "public" beach. Everyone knew one another and would meet up, year after year. Some of us stayed between the rows of cabins all af­ternoon, only crossing the sand in order to bathe. For these cabins were raised on a pebbly part of the beach. A major effort was needed to cross these rounded shingles without succumbing to the pain. This was particularly so at the start of the season when everyone would be contorting their chests seeking some balance, like a wonky puppet: but with a bit of practice we began to run without feeling the pain at all.

In front of the maritime baths, in the sea, there was also some wreckage; and many are the Havrais, big or small, who remember their feet sinking into the soft sand which surrounded the metal parts sticking out from this ship, and often getting wounded. As for me, this is where I learnt to swim. One day, when I was bathing in the sea here, I felt this sensation of moving and of the metal bits below my feet. I pulled them up quickly and went horizontal, fleeing this man-trap in an improvised breaststroke.

So the cabins were often the meetingplace of for groups of young people for whom beach games were the major activity of the summer. Volleyball teams were formed by the older ones; and the younger set were admiring of their elders in all this, dreaming sometimes that their favourite boy or girl would cast a glance in their direction! The awakening of amorous feelings would lead you to find the target of your dreams on the beach, only for that one to be pursued and replaced the following year by someone newly arrived or a regular who, during the winter, had been transformed into a butterfly.

The beach was the location of the first kind of independence, even if it was still under the eyes of the adults; the first emotional stirrings, the first meals taken in wooden-plank cafés, long discussions, swapping and lending books and records .... So, growing up! With us, however, beach times meant always time spent on the beach, not engaging in card games or aperitifs sipped with cabin neighbours. Snippets of news exchanged, but not much more. Once we had undressed in the cabin we would pick up our chairs and take them down to the water's edge. That way we kept our traditions of the "people's beach".

For the beach operated a very compartmentalised social hierarchy: at Sainte-Adresse was the exclusive Régates et les Courlis Club where the Dionysian families gathered and their young went to very sought-after bashes; then there was Galetville, more downmarket, with its maritime baths where you could hire bathing costumes. The closer you got to the sea-wall the less prestigious were the social strata.

When my parents' friends left Le Havre we bought their cabin; and as soon as it became possible we moved to the first row, on the sea-wall side, because my father wanted to see the entrance to the port with its constant movement of ships. The melting-pot nature of this location satisfied us. There was a mixing of social groups, a diversity of cultures, with everyone preserving a personal brand of belonging. Some people wore bathing costumes; others not: some came in groups of families, with plenty of children running about, chattering and crying. One's gaze arrived sometimes on a piece of sculpture; every colour was there, giving the place a simple quality of harmony and toleration. This concoction led us, in those days, to call our neck of the beach "Abidjan Beach".

And this is where we are still located today. The structure, which will soon be fifty years old, is holding up well. Each generation follows on; and the baton is now held by the younger ones and their children; but the games are the same. However, there's no more portable stove allowing you to have tea facing the sea in September after a robust dip in the sea (that was the best!); no more canoes; no more quality equipment, because robberies have become too common; no more sand, really! There still remains the pleasure of bathing in the sea; of chips in cardboard boxes; of ice-creams melting in the sun; of light reading and of the book left open, laid on the bathing-towel while you take big gulps from your bottle of water ...

-Laurence Le Cieux

The title of the book, which inspired this website, "Colours on the Beach", is connected to Karel Martens' large-scale commission of the same name in Le Havre, France (2017), in which he devised a coded rhythm with which to paint the traditionally white beach cabins that form a distinct part of the Le Havre’s cityscape.
